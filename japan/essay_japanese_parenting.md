# Raising Children the Japanese Way: A Guide to Traditional Japanese Parenting for Modern Parents

---

## A Note Before You Begin

This guide has a problem to confess upfront. For decades, Western observers have looked at Japanese children — their patience in school corridors, their self-sufficiency on commutes, their empathy toward peers — and concluded that Japan must hold some parenting secret the West has lost. Books have been written. Articles have gone viral. Netflix aired a show about toddlers running their first errand alone and millions of people watched it with something between admiration and longing.

Most of what these observers concluded was true. And most of what they concluded was wrong — not wrong about the children, but wrong about *why*. They saw outcomes and assumed portable techniques. They saw cultural practices and assumed individual attitudes. They saw an idealized middle-class Japan and missed the exam-driven anxiety, the maternal pressure, the tripling of child abuse reports over a single decade.

This guide tries to do something harder than a listicle: to give you the philosophical skeleton behind Japanese parenting traditions, to treat the original concepts with enough care that their spirit remains intact, and to be honest with you about what can travel across cultures and what cannot. Traditional Japanese parenting is not a program you can install. It is a way of understanding what a child is.

Let's start there.

---

## Part One: What a Child Is

In the dominant Western developmental tradition — shaped by Locke, Rousseau, Piaget, and, more recently, the positive psychology movement — a child is a becoming individual. The job of parenting is to support the emergence of an autonomous self: to cultivate independence, self-expression, confidence, and the capacity for self-determination. The child arrives, in this frame, as a project.

Traditional Japanese culture begins somewhere else entirely. The child arrives as a relationship.

This distinction is not merely philosophical. It shapes everything: how you hold a baby, how you arrange a bedroom, how you handle a tantrum, what you say when a child succeeds, what you say when a child fails. In Japan, the infant is not a bounded self who will gradually learn to belong to others. The infant is already constituted by belonging — to mother, to family, to household, to group. Development does not mean moving from dependence to independence; it means learning to inhabit, skillfully and graciously, the web of relationships you were born into.

Psychologists Shinobu Kitayama and Hazel Markus, in foundational cross-cultural research, called this the "interdependent self-construal." In a striking empirical finding published in *Frontiers in Psychology* (2010), they found that the strongest predictor of wellbeing and health was personal control in the United States — but *the absence of relational strain* in Japan. In other words: Japanese people flourish not by achieving inner mastery but by being in good relationship with others. Parenting, in this light, is not the development of an individual capacity. It is the cultivation of a relational self.

Keep this in mind throughout the guide. Every practice that follows — the co-sleeping, the non-intervention, the group cleaning, the restraint in praise — makes sense from within this frame, and can look inexplicable or even harmful from outside it.

## Part Two: The Philosophy in Three Concepts

### Amae (甘え) — The Strength in Dependency

In 1971, the Japanese psychiatrist Doi Takeo published *Amae no Kozo* (*The Anatomy of Dependence*), a book that became one of the most widely discussed works of Japanese social psychology. Doi's argument was deceptively simple. There is a mode of being in relationships — he called it *amae* — that Western developmental psychology had systematically misread as pathology.

Amae, Doi wrote, is "to depend and presume upon another's benevolence." It is the infant's state of absolute trust that it will be held, fed, and loved without needing to earn or reciprocate that love. Western developmental theory, shaped by Bowlby and later Ainsworth, treated this state as a transitional phase to be resolved through "secure attachment" and then graduated into independence. The goal is always to move the child *through* the dependent state toward something more autonomous.

Doi's observation was that Japan does not treat this as a phase to be passed through. Amae persists into adulthood. The same trust that characterizes the mother-infant relationship is replicated — in modulated, contextually appropriate forms — in teacher-student relationships, between colleagues, between friends. It is not, Doi argued, immaturity. It is a sophisticated form of relational intelligence: knowing how to lean on another person without exploiting them, and how to receive another's leaning without resentment.

For parents, the practical implication is significant: the Japanese parenting instinct is to *respond* to a young child's needs with fullness and warmth rather than to begin early training in self-sufficiency. A baby who is picked up immediately when it cries is not being "spoiled" in the Japanese frame; it is being shown that the world is responsive, that belonging is real, that it can trust its relationships. This is not permissiveness without structure. It is attachment before autonomy — not instead of it.

*A caveat for modern parents:* Doi's critics, including the psychologist Per Gjerde writing in the *American Psychologist* (2001), argued that amae's importance was overstated and the evidence for its uniqueness to Japan was largely anecdotal. More pointedly, critics have noted that amae guidance has historically been aimed disproportionately at women, carrying expectations of yielding behavior that can reinforce gender inequality. Use the concept as a philosophical corrective to over-early independence-training — not as a reason to structure everything around maternal indulgence.

---

### Sunao (素直) — Gentle and Open, Not Obedient

If amae describes what the parent gives, *sunao* describes what the parent hopes to cultivate: a child who is gentle, receptive, cooperative — open to the world and to others.

The psychologist Hiroshi Azuma, who co-edited the foundational comparative volume *Child Development and Education in Japan* (W.H. Freeman, 1986), described a sunao child as "a good participant in group activities, a good listener to adults, a good replicator of society's norms and standards." This sounds like obedience. Azuma was insistent that it is not. A sunao child, he wrote, "has not yielded his or her personal autonomy for the sake of cooperation; cooperation does not suggest giving up the self... it implies that working with others is the *appropriate way of expressing and enhancing the self*."

This distinction matters enormously. Western parenting discourse tends to frame the development of autonomy and the development of social cooperation as a tension to be managed — give the child room to be themselves, but teach them to share and take turns. In the sunao framework, this is a false opposition. There is no "true self" that exists prior to and in tension with relationships. The self *emerges through* cooperative engagement with others. Teaching a child to listen well, to read others carefully, to participate fluidly in group activity — this is not taming the child. It is developing the child's deepest capacities.

The key pedagogical mechanism for cultivating sunao is *wakaraseru* — a Japanese verb meaning "to help someone understand." The goal is not compliance without understanding. Japanese parents and educators, as Takie Sugiyama Lebra documented in her foundational anthropological study *Japanese Patterns of Behavior* (University of Hawaii Press, 1976), believed that "compliance without a willing desire on the part of the child was of little or no value." The child must *understand why*. This is patient teaching, not command-and-control.

*For modern parents:* The sunao orientation invites you to examine whether your child-rearing aims at producing an independent self or a relationally skilled one — and whether these two actually need to be in conflict. It also suggests a practical discipline: explain more, command less.

---

### Ganbaru (頑張る) — Persist, and Mean It

*Ganbaru* and its imperative form *ganbare* (keep going; you can do it) are among the most pervasive expressions in Japanese daily life. They are applied to children from toddlerhood onward. They are said to athletes, students, employees, friends recovering from illness, and to entire cities recovering from natural disaster. In the aftermath of the 2011 Tōhoku earthquake, *ganbare Nihon* — "persist, Japan" — was everywhere.

The concept differs from what the American psychologist Angela Duckworth famously called "grit" in one crucial dimension. Duckworth's grit is individualist and goal-directed: passion plus perseverance toward a self-chosen long-term objective. *Ganbaru* is fundamentally relational. One persists partly because others are watching. Partly because letting the group down is unacceptable. Partly because the struggle itself — not just the outcome — is understood to have moral weight. The Japanese proverb *nana korobi ya oki* (七転び八起き) — "fall seven times, rise eight" — captures this. The falling is not failure. The rising is not triumph. The act of rising, again and again, *is the point*.

Lois Peak's landmark ethnographic study *Learning to Go to School in Japan* (University of California Press, 1991) documented how Japanese preschool teachers deliberately withheld help when children struggled, treating the struggle as a developmental gift. The teachers described their role not as making things easy but as "giving children the chance to try hard." Ganbaru opportunities are engineered. A modern parent who rushes in too quickly to smooth every difficulty is, in this frame, doing the child a disservice — not from cruelty but from depriving them of something they need.

*A caveat that must be stated:* Ganbaru can shade into something darker. Japan's epidemic of karoshi — death from overwork — has roots in the same cultural logic applied to adult labor. The social pressure dimension of ganbaru can suppress legitimate self-advocacy and make it difficult for children (and adults) to say "this is too much." Used well, the concept teaches that effort has moral value independent of outcome. Used poorly, it becomes a mechanism for overlooking genuine suffering.

## Part Three: The Social Architecture

### Wa (和) and Shudan Seikatsu — Living as a Group

The Japanese value of *wa* — harmony, concord — is traceable to Prince Shotoku's 604 CE constitutional document: "Harmony is to be valued." It is not a soft concept. It is a practice, a competence, something that must be actively cultivated and repeatedly repaired.

In the preschool years, wa takes the form of *shudan seikatsu* — "group life." Joseph Tobin, David Wu, and Dana Davidson, in their landmark comparative ethnography *Preschool in Three Cultures* (Yale University Press, 1989; revised University of Chicago Press, 2009), filmed a typical day at Japanese, Chinese, and American preschools, then showed the footage to teachers and parents across all three countries. What American observers found most alarming about the Japanese preschool was its class size: up to 30 children per teacher. Surely this was a resource constraint?

Japanese educators had an entirely different explanation. Large groups were deliberate. A child in a small, closely teacher-managed group has most of their conflicts resolved by adult intervention. A child in a large group of peers must learn to navigate social complexity themselves. The teacher's role, Japanese educators explained, was to "stay on the fringes of children's play" — watching, available, but not managing. When conflicts arose, teachers waited. Only by working through peer conflict themselves, they explained, can children "grow to become harmonious, empathetic citizens."

This is one of the most transferable insights in the Japanese parenting tradition: adults who intervene too quickly in children's social conflicts inadvertently teach children that adults, not they themselves, are responsible for their social world. The Japanese preschool goal, as Tobin's informants articulated it, is to help children "integrate the individual and group dimensions of self." The goal is not a submissive group-member. It is someone who can hold their own individuality within the demands of collective life.

*A caveat:* Wa has a shadow. The pressure for harmony that discourages conflict also creates the conditions for *ijime* — bullying — in which children who deviate from group norms become targets. The drive for harmony can suppress individual voice in ways that are genuinely harmful, particularly for children who are different in ways they cannot change. No honest account of this tradition can leave this out.

---

### Uchi/Soto (内/外) — Teaching Children Where They Are

Japanese children are taught, from early childhood, that their behavior will and should change depending on context. *Uchi* (inside, home group) and *soto* (outside, the public and stranger world) define one of the most fundamental structural principles in Japanese social life.

Within the uchi sphere — immediate family, close friends, classroom group — informality, emotional expression, and the leaning of amae are permitted and even expected. Within the soto sphere — formal contexts, strangers, public spaces — restraint, politeness (including the use of honorific *keigo* language), and careful self-presentation are required. The child learns to code-switch not as hypocrisy but as competence.

Takie Sugiyama Lebra, whose *Japanese Patterns of Behavior* (1976) remains the canonical anthropological account, argued that uchi/soto is not simply about manners. It reflects a deeper truth: the self is genuinely different in different relational contexts. What it means to be *me* depends on who I am with. This is not a distortion of authentic identity; it is a sophisticated understanding of how social identity actually works.

For modern parents, this framework invites a reconsideration of what "being yourself" means. Western parenting culture tends to emphasize the singular, consistent self — help your child know who they are, and be that person everywhere. The Japanese tradition suggests a different kind of authenticity: knowing how to be genuinely present in each relational context, rather than importing one relational mode into all contexts regardless of fit.

## Part Four: The Daily Practices

### Soine (添い寝) — Sleeping Together

The Japanese sleeping arrangement has a name: *soine* (添い寝), "to lie down together." It typically takes the form of the *kawa no ji* (川の字) — the kanji character for "river," representing child between parents, like water between riverbanks. The philosophical foundation is *anshinkan* — a deep sense of security and contentment cultivated through physical proximity and touch.

James McKenna, the world's leading anthropological researcher on mother-infant co-sleeping, documents that Japan maintains one of the lowest SIDS rates in the world: 0.2–0.3 per 1,000 births versus approximately 0.5 in the US. A landmark 1999 study in *Archives of Pediatric & Adolescent Medicine* found that 59% of Japanese co-sleeping children slept with parents three or more nights per week, and that Japanese co-sleeping was not associated with the same sleep disruptions found in American co-sleeping families — suggesting that cultural context fundamentally shapes the practice's outcomes. A 2024 cross-sectional survey found 89% of Japanese caregivers still practiced bedsharing.

McKenna's formulation is worth holding: "It is not necessarily bed-sharing, but how it is practiced, that can be dangerous." Japanese co-sleeping typically occurs with breastfeeding mothers who are non-smokers, on firm futon surfaces, without alcohol. These conditions matter.

*For modern parents:* The case for soine is fundamentally the case for physical closeness as a relational practice, not merely a sleeping arrangement. For parents who choose not to bed-share, the underlying principle — that nighttime closeness builds the anshinkan (sense of security) that Doi's amae framework describes — can still inform how they approach night wakings, settling, and the emotional texture of bedtime routines.

---

### Soji (掃除) — The Children Clean

Every day in Japanese elementary and secondary schools, a 20-minute block is reserved for *gakko soji* — school cleaning. Students sweep, mop, dust, and clean shared spaces, including toilets. There are no hired janitors doing this work. Sixth-graders teach first-graders the techniques. The practice traces its philosophical roots approximately 500 years to Zen Buddhist temple training, in which maintaining physical cleanliness was understood as inseparable from mental discipline.

The Ministry of Education links soji explicitly to moral development: "acquisition of well-mannered behaviour in the social context, respect for law and order within the society, and a sense of social duty and responsibility." Educator Yutaka Okihara put it more directly: "No work, not even the dirty work of cleaning, is too low for a student; all should share equally in common tasks."

What soji transmits is something that cannot be taught in a classroom: the understanding that the shared world requires shared maintenance. The child who cleans her own classroom is implicated in its upkeep. She is not a consumer of a service; she is a participant in a community. This is a concrete, embodied version of the wa orientation — the idea that belonging means contribution, not just membership.

*For modern parents:* You do not need a school cleaning schedule to adopt this principle. Involving children in household work from young ages — not as chores assigned for reward, but as participation in a shared household — transmits the same understanding. The goal is not clean floors but the normalization of the idea that shared spaces have shared responsibilities.

---

### Mimamoru (見守る) — Watching Without Intervening

One of the most challenging practices in Japanese early childhood education for Western parents to absorb is *mimamoru* — "watching and protecting" — a pedagogical stance in which adults deliberately refrain from intervening in children's struggles, conflicts, and exploration.

Associate Professor Fuminori Nakatsubo at Hiroshima University, writing in the *Early Childhood Education Journal* (2021), documented mimamoru as an implicit standard across Japanese early childhood settings. His counterintuitive focus was physical conflicts between children: why do Japanese educators not intervene when children fight? His finding: "Children learn through their exploration of autonomy under the protection of adults." The adult's presence provides safety. Their non-intervention provides the developmental opportunity.

This philosophy extends well beyond physical conflicts. Japanese parents and educators traditionally avoid rushing in to solve problems children are capable of working through themselves, allow children to experience the natural consequences of their choices, and resist the impulse to smooth every difficulty. The concept of *jiritsu* — self-reliance — is cultivated not through independence training but through the experience of struggling with difficulty and discovering one can get through it.

The parallel to Carol Dweck's research on praise and mindset is striking. Dweck's landmark Stanford studies found that praising children for effort rather than innate ability produced dramatically better outcomes: when students were praised for their effort, 90% chose the challenging new task they could learn from, versus ability-praised students who opted for easier tasks to protect their reputations. The Japanese restraint in ability-focused praise and the embrace of productive struggle anticipates Dweck's empirical findings by decades.

*A necessary caveat:* Mimamoru is not neglect, and it is not passive. It requires the adult to be fully present and attentive — available when genuinely needed — while resisting the impulse to manage what the child can manage themselves. The distinction between mimamoru and harmful non-response requires judgment. Some children need more active scaffolding than this philosophy provides.

---

### Kosodate (子育て) — It Was Never Just the Parents

*Kosodate* — child-rearing — was, for most of Japanese history, not a parental task. It was a community one.

In Japan's Edo period (1603–1868), child-rearing was shared among fathers, mothers, grandparents, older siblings, and wet nurses. Community ceremonies — *kamioki*, *himotoki*, *shichi-go-san* — publicly marked each developmental milestone, binding the child's upbringing to the social fabric of the village. As historian Ohta Motoko (Wako University) documented, parents became conscious of their educational responsibility precisely *as* a social responsibility, not a private one. Children were *kodakara* — treasure — belonging not just to their parents but to the community that held them.

Associate Professor Atsuko Saito of Sophia University argues that the contemporary assumption — that mothers alone bear child-rearing responsibility — is both historically recent and biologically anomalous. "Human infants are uniquely immature compared to other primates and require extended care," she notes. Outside assistance is "biologically necessary rather than optional." The isolation of mothers within nuclear family units, she argues, is not a natural arrangement but a historically specific and structurally produced one. Japan's catastrophically low birth rate — under 800,000 births in 2022 — is increasingly linked by scholars and policymakers to precisely this collapse of communal child-rearing structures.

*For modern parents:* The kosodate tradition is a direct challenge to the privatization of parenting that characterizes much of the modern world. You cannot recreate a village from scratch. But you can actively resist the isolation that modern parenting often imposes: build networks with other parents, accept help without shame, recognize that a child raised with multiple caring adults develops differently than one raised in a sealed parental dyad. The village is not a luxury. It is, in Saito's framing, a biological necessity.

## Part Five: What to Borrow — and What Not To

### What Travels Well

Several elements of the Japanese parenting tradition are genuinely portable — not as complete practices but as orienting principles that can reshape how you approach your daily parenting work.

**The relational starting point.** The most important philosophical shift is the simplest: beginning from the premise that your child is a relational being, not a project of individualization. This does not mean you stop honoring your child's individual needs and personality. It means you add a question to your developmental lens: not only "is my child developing as an individual?" but "is my child learning to live well with others?"

**Effort over ability.** The mimamoru orientation and the ganbaru culture, combined with Dweck's confirmatory research, give you clear practical guidance: praise process, not product; allow struggle; resist the impulse to rescue; communicate that effort has intrinsic value regardless of outcome.

**Explanation over command.** The wakaraseru approach — helping children understand the *why* rather than simply requiring compliance — produces not just better short-term cooperation but children who internalize values rather than performing them under observation.

**Shared responsibility.** Involve children in household maintenance, meal preparation, and the care of shared spaces from young ages. Not as chores with extrinsic rewards, but as participation in a shared world.

**Physical closeness.** Whatever your sleeping arrangements, the anshinkan principle — the cultivation of deep bodily security through consistent physical closeness — can inform the texture of your daily interactions: carried rather than pushed, held rather than placed, present rather than at a distance.

---

### What Does Not Travel Simply

Intellectual honesty requires naming what does not transfer:

**Children commuting alone.** This is perhaps the most famous Japanese childhood image in Western popular culture, amplified by the Netflix show *Old Enough!* (which depicts toddlers running errands independently). What Western coverage almost never mentions is what journalist Henry Grabar documented in *Slate* (2022): "Japanese kids ages 10 and 11 make just 15 percent of weekday trips with a parent" — but this is enabled by school-mandated routes (*tsūgakuro*), compulsory neighborhood travel groups (*han*), GPS-equipped school bags, community volunteer safety networks, and time-window gate rules that structure every journey. As Nippon.com noted dryly: "They are not doing so in an unsupervised environment." You cannot replicate the attitude without the infrastructure.

**The full group dynamics of *wa*.** The shudan seikatsu philosophy of Japanese preschools functions within a full cultural scaffolding: extended family networks, senpai/kohai structures, community institutions, shared cultural norms about conflict and harmony. Transplanting the surface feature — a less intervention-heavy parenting style — without the surrounding structure can produce children who simply feel unsupported rather than children who develop peer negotiation skills.

**Haji (恥) — shame-based social regulation.** The use of shame as a social regulator — linking a child's behavior to family and community reputation — is deeply embedded in Japanese moral socialization. It is not straightforwardly transferable. The psychological research of June Price Tangney, summarized in *Shame and Guilt* (Guilford Press, 2002), found that shame-proneness (unlike guilt-proneness) is associated with poorer emotional outcomes, greater aggression, and depression in children. The specific cultural architecture that makes haji functional in Japan — the uchi/soto framework, the group belonging, the mutuality of social obligation — is not present in most Western contexts.

## Part Six: The Shadow

No honest guide to Japanese parenting can conclude without acknowledging what the idealized image obscures.

The *kyōiku mama* — the "education mother" — is a real and documented phenomenon. The term emerged in Japan in the early 1960s to describe mothers who subordinate their children's social and emotional development to relentless academic preparation, driven by Japan's hyper-competitive credentialing system. This is not a distortion of Japanese parenting values. As researcher Rachael Burke documented in *He Kupu* (2014), it is an intensification of the *ryōsai kenbō* ("good wife, wise mother") ideology that Meiji-era modernization installed and that remains structurally in place. The *kyōiku mama* was condemned by Japanese psychologists for contributing to school phobia, childhood anxiety, and teenage suicide — and simultaneously praised by Reagan-era American education officials as a heroic model to emulate. Marie Thorsten, in *Superhuman Japan* (Routledge, 2012), identified this as a pattern: Western observers project whatever they currently idealize onto Japanese motherhood, regardless of what Japanese society itself is saying about it.

Child abuse reports in Japan tripled from approximately 34,000 to over 103,000 cases in a single decade, documented by Aya Goto and colleagues in the *Journal of Epidemiology* (2020). The declining birth rate — now among the lowest in the developed world — is in part a symptom of the crushing burden placed on parents, particularly mothers, within a system that has stripped away communal support without replacing it. The harmonious, self-reliant, group-oriented Japanese child of Western imagination is, substantially, a middle-class urban child whose mother may have stopped working and whose family may be quietly drowning in the pressure to perform the ideal.

Jennifer Lansford, reviewing cross-cultural parenting research in the *Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry* (2021), offered the sharpest warning: "Findings about cultural differences from the past may no longer be found today." Japan has changed. What researchers documented in the 1970s and 1980s — the era that generated most of what Western culture "knows" about Japanese parenting — is not identical to Japan now.

Use this tradition with respect and with clear eyes. It contains genuine wisdom about what children are, how they grow, and what they need from the adults who raise them. It also contains pressures, distortions, and structural inequities that no thoughtful parent should wish to import wholesale.

The wisdom is real. The spirit is worth recovering. But the spirit was never in the techniques.

---

## Conclusion: The Heart of the Tradition

Strip away the specifics — the futon arrangements, the cleaning rotations, the non-intervening teachers — and something coherent remains at the center of the traditional Japanese parenting vision.

A child is born into a web of relationships that constitute its world. The work of childhood is not to escape that web in order to become free, but to learn to inhabit it with skill, grace, and genuine care. The parent's role is not to sculpt an individual but to hold and transmit a way of being in the world — one that takes seriously the claims of others, that finds meaning in collective life, that treats effort as intrinsically valuable and difficulty as necessary.

Takie Sugiyama Lebra's foundational image from *Japanese Patterns of Behavior* stays with you: the Japanese individual does not experience uchi and soto, or the self's different facets in different relationships, as fragmentation or inauthenticity. Each context has its own genuine obligations and its own genuine self. There is no single core self beneath the social roles. The roles, inhabited fully and with care, *are* the self.

This is a different vision of human flourishing than the one that currently dominates Western parenting culture. It is not necessarily better. But it is genuinely different — and for parents who suspect that the relentless cultivation of individual autonomy may be leaving something out, it offers a profound and coherent alternative.

*Ganbatte.* Keep going.
