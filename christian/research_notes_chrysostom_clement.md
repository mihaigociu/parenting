## 1. Saint John Chrysostom — *Address on Vainglory and the Right Way for Parents to Bring Up Their Children* (*De Inani Gloria et de Educandis Liberis*), c. 390 CE

### Textual Status and Access

The standard English translation is M. L. W. Laistner's, published in *Christianity and Pagan Culture in the Later Roman Empire* (Cornell University Press, 1951). The text survives in two manuscripts, *Parisinus graecus* 764 and *Lesbos* 42, copied in the late tenth or early eleventh century. Its absence from the standard Migne *Patrologia* (due to authenticity questions settled by the German scholar Franz Schulte in 1914) kept it underexplored. The Laistner translation is partially available via the Internet Archive; a PDF scan of the Laistner book is at archive.org/details/christianitypaga00lais. Key passages were confirmed via multiple secondary sites and the De Wet (2014) academic article, which quotes the Laistner translation directly with section numbers.

### Summary of Main Educational / Parenting Ideas

Chrysostom's treatise divides into two main sections: the first (§1–15) attacks the pagan euergetic system and the way secular culture corrupts children by prioritizing wealth, fame, and rhetorical glory over virtue. The second and larger section is a positive program for Christian parenting, addressed primarily to the father, who is cast as the primary moral guardian of the household rather than a delegator to hired tutors. The child's soul is treated as supremely malleable in early years and must be shaped like wax or sculpted like a statue before it hardens. Chrysostom recommends a comprehensive program of sensory control (governing what the child sees, hears, touches, smells, and says), narrative formation (telling biblical stories at the dinner table with repetition), physical austerity (avoiding luxury and servants), and emotional guidance (training patience but defending others courageously). The treatise moves developmentally through stages — infant, young child, older boy, youth — with increasingly sophisticated spiritual demands at each stage.

### Structure of the Essay

- **§1–15**: Critique of vainglory in civic and educational life; pagan euergetism as corrupting force; neglect of children's souls.
- **§16–38**: Role and responsibility of parents; the soul as city/wax/pearl; the five gates of the senses; the father as moral king; early formation practices.
- **§39–67**: Scriptural storytelling; dinner-table pedagogy; specific biblical narratives (Jacob and Esau); training in patience, chastity, temperance; avoidance of theatre and bad company.
- **§68–90**: Adolescence and youth; chastity training; the mother's role with daughters; the danger of luxury and servants; final exhortation.

### Key Passages and Quotes

*Section numbers follow the Sources Chrétiennes edition (Malingrey, 1972), as used by De Wet (2014) and Christou (2018). All quotes from Laistner translation.*

**On neglect of children's souls (§18):**
> "In our own day every man takes the greatest pains to train his boy in the arts and in literature and speech. But to exercise this child's soul in virtue, to that no man any longer pays heed."

**On parents as sculptors (§22):**
> "Just as we see artists fashioning their paintings and statues with great precision, so we must care for these wondrous statues of ours."

**On raising a Christian athlete (§19):**
> "Raise up an athlete for Christ and teach him though he is living in the world to be reverent from his earliest youth."

**The wax metaphor (§20–21):**
> "If good precepts are impressed on the soul while it is yet tender, no man will be able to destroy them when they have set firm, even as does a waxen seal."

**The pearl metaphor (§21):**
> "They say that pearls when first they are collected are but water...he places the drop on his hand; and moving it with a gentle rotating movement...he shapes it skilfully and renders it perfectly round."

**The city metaphor (§24–25):**
> "Regard thyself as king ruling over a city which is the soul of thy son; for the soul is in truth a city."

**The five gates / senses (§25):**
> "The outer walls and four gates, the senses, are built...the gates are the eyes, the tongue, the hearing, the sense of smell...It is through these gates that thoughts are corrupted or rightly guided."

**On storytelling at dinner (§39–40):**
> "Tell him this story one evening at supper. Let his mother repeat the same tale; then, when he has heard it often, ask him too, saying: 'Tell me the story,' so that he may be eager to imitate you."

**On the power of narrative (§44):**
> "If a story can so master the children's soul that it is thought worthy of belief, the veritable truth, it will surely enthrall them and fill them with great awe."

**On the need for austerity (§16):**
> "There is need for a strict tutor to direct the boy, there is no need for gold."

**On the theatre (§56, 59):**
> "Never send your son to the theater that he may not suffer utter corruption through his ears and eyes."

**On teaching patience and justice (§66):**
> "Let us train boys from earliest childhood to be patient when they suffer wrongs themselves, but, if they see another being wronged, to sally forth courageously and aid the sufferer in fitting measure."

**On the father as lawgiver (§67):**
> "If the laws are transgressed, he will be stern and unyielding; if they are observed, he will be gracious and kind and will bestow many rewards on the boy."

**On the mother's role with daughters (§90):**
> "Let his mother learn to train her daughter by these precepts, to guide her away from extravagance and personal adornment and all other such vanities that are the mark of harlots."

**On the goal of formation (§8):**
> "Form the soul of thy son aright, and all the rest will be added hereafter."

**On the naming of children:**
> "So let the name of the saints enter our homes through the naming of our children, to train not only the child but the father..."

**On the priority of formation:**
> "Let everything take second place to our care of our children, our bringing them up to the discipline and instruction of the Lord."

### Specific Practices Recommended

1. **Control of the senses from birth**: Guard all five gates — eyes, ears, tongue, nose, touch — against corrupting input. No obscene speech, no unseemly sights, no soft music, no luxurious food.
2. **Storytelling at the dinner table**: Each evening, tell a biblical narrative; the father tells it, the mother repeats it, then the child is asked to retell it. Chrysostom specifies Jacob and Esau (self-control vs. gluttony) and Cain and Abel (envy and murder) from Genesis.
3. **Exclusion from theatre and pagan entertainment**: Theatre is "utter corruption through ears and eyes."
4. **Physical austerity**: Do not let the child be waited on by servants; avoid extravagant clothing and food. No wine until late adolescence.
5. **Naming children after saints**: Establishes a patron and a model; educates the father himself.
6. **Teaching Scripture actively**: Memorize psalms and pray.
7. **The father as lawgiver**: Sets rules with clear consequences — reward for observance, firm but not cruel punishment for transgression.
8. **Staged development**: Differentiated practices for infants, young children, older boys, and youth.

### Role of Father vs. Mother

The father is the primary pedagogical figure — the "king" of the city-soul, the lawgiver, the one who tells the stories. His responsibility is the formation of sons. The mother reinforces the father's teaching and retells his stories; she is specifically entrusted with daughters in §90. Chrysostom addresses fathers directly throughout most of the treatise.

### Warnings about Vainglory, Wealth, and Bad Company

The opening sections (§1–15) constitute a sustained critique of Roman euergetism — civic benefaction driven by desire for public honor — as the root corruption of education. Parents who spend on rhetorical tutors while neglecting their children's souls are guilty of the same vainglory. Wealth is a positive danger: luxury softens, servants create dependency, fine clothes train toward vanity. Bad company — especially at the theatre and among peers who use foul language — corrupts through the senses.

### Internal Tensions and Caveats

1. **Gender asymmetry**: Almost entirely addressed to fathers raising sons. The formation of daughters (§90) is brief and assigned entirely to mothers.
2. **Social location**: The treatise assumes a household wealthy enough to have servants — addressed to urban elites, not the majority of the late Roman population.
3. **Authenticity and transmission**: Absent from the standard Migne *Patrologia* due to authenticity doubts; settled by Schulte (1914). The *Sources Chrétiennes* critical edition (Malingrey, 1972) is now the standard.
4. **Dialogue with Hellenism**: Chrysostom draws extensively on classical rhetorical and philosophical pedagogy (Platonic soul-formation, Stoic self-control, Aristotelian habituation) while ostensibly rejecting the pagan educational system. Roskam (2014) argues the first section is more deeply engaged with pagan civic culture than it appears.

## 2. Clement of Alexandria — *Paedagogus* (*The Instructor*), c. 198 CE

### Textual Status and Access

- **New Advent** (Book I, ANF translation): https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/02091.htm
- **Early Christian Writings**: http://www.earlychristianwritings.com/text/clement-instructor-book1.html
- **Documenta Catholica Omnia** (Schaff translation PDF): documentacatholicaomnia.eu

The ANF version (New Advent and Early Christian Writings) uses "Instructor" for *Paedagogus*. The modern scholarly translation is Simon P. Wood's *Christ the Educator* (Fathers of the Church series, CUA Press, 1954), preferred for accuracy over the older ANF.

### Summary of Main Educational Ideas

Clement's *Paedagogus* is the second volume of his trilogy (following the *Protrepticus*, an evangelistic work, and preceding the *Stromateis*, a work of advanced theological instruction). Its central claim: the Logos of God — Christ incarnate — is the universal Pedagogue of the entire human race. The *Paedagogus* is explicitly distinguished from a teacher (*didaskalos*): the Pedagogue does not primarily convey doctrine but forms the soul, corrects habits, and heals the passions, making the person capable of receiving deeper instruction later. Book I establishes theological and Christological foundations; Books II and III apply them to practical behavior (eating, drinking, dress, sex, sleep, household management). The goal is virtue understood as likeness to God — ontological participation in the divine nature, not merely ethical rectitude. Clement synthesizes Stoic moral philosophy (drawing explicitly on Musonius Rufus), Platonic idealism, Jewish Scripture, and Christian theology.

### Structure of the Three Books

- **Book I**: Theological foundations. Who is the Instructor? What is his method? Why are believers called "children"? Chapters 1–4 establish the Instructor's identity and method; Chapters 5–6 explain the theological significance of childhood; Chapters 7–9 identify Jesus as the Instructor through Scripture.
- **Book II**: Specific applications — food and drink, feasts, ointments, behavior at symposia, laughter, speech, sleep, sexual conduct.
- **Book III**: Further applications — adornment, jewelry, dress, baths, exercise, footwear; a closing hymn to Christ the Educator.

### What Clement Means by "Christ as Pedagogue"

The Pedagogue is Christ as Logos acting in the practical, formative, moral dimension of Christian life. Clement distinguishes three levels of discourse (Chapter 1): hortatory discourse shapes *habits*; preceptive discourse governs *actions*; persuasive discourse heals *passions*. The Pedagogue operates across all three, especially in forming habits and healing passions — the pre-rational dimensions of the soul. The Logos-as-Didaskalos operates at a higher level: conveying revealed doctrine, guiding toward gnosis. This means human parents and teachers participate in Christ's own pedagogical work at the creaturely level — correcting behavior, forming good habits, guiding away from passion.

### Distinction Between *Paideia* and *Didaskalia*

Clement's opening chapter is the locus classicus for this distinction in early Christian thought. *Paideia* (the Pedagogue's work) is prior to and preparatory for *didaskalia* (the teacher's work). You cannot receive doctrine until your character is formed; you cannot understand the Word until your passions are healed.

### Key Passages and Quotes

*(All directly fetched from newadvent.org/fathers/02091.htm and earlychristianwritings.com/text/clement-instructor-book1.html — ANF translation)*

**On the Pedagogue's aim (Chapter 1):**
> "The Instructor being practical, not theoretical, His aim is thus to improve the soul, not to teach, and to train it up to a virtuous, not to an intellectual life."

**On the physician metaphor (Chapter 1):**
> "As, then, for those of us who are diseased in body a physician is required, so also those who are diseased in soul require a pædagogue to cure our maladies; and then a teacher, to train and guide the soul to all requisite knowledge when it is made able to admit the revelation of the Word."

**On Christ as Instructor (Chapter 2):**
> "Now, O you, my children, our Instructor is like His Father God, whose son He is, sinless, blameless, and with a soul devoid of passion; God in the form of man, stainless, the minister of His Father's will, the Word who is God, who is in the Father, who is at the Father's right hand, and with the form of God is God."

**On assimilation to God as the goal (Chapter 2):**
> "He is to us a spotless image; to Him we are to try with all our might to assimilate our souls."

**On the image-and-likeness mandate (Chapter 3):**
> "That we may...perform the works of the Master according to His similitude, and so fulfil what Scripture says as to our being made in His image and likeness."

**On Jesus explicitly named as Instructor (Chapter 7):**
> "The Word, then, who leads the children to salvation, is appropriately called the Instructor (Pædagogue). Our Instructor is the holy God Jesus, the Word, who is the guide of all humanity."

**On the childlike quality of believers (Chapter 5):**
> "The child is therefore gentle...more tender, delicate, and simple, guileless, and destitute of hypocrisy."

**On reproof as surgery (Chapter 8):**
> "Reproof is, as it were, the surgery of the passions of the soul...dissolving the callosities of the passions, and purging the impurities of the lewdness of the life."

**On the need for a guide (Chapter 4):**
> "Without a shepherd, neither can sheep nor any other animal live, nor children without a tutor, nor domestics without a master."

### Theological Claim and Its Effect on Human Parents

Because Christ is the universal Pedagogue, every human act of formation participates in a divine economy. God's love — His having made us in His image, His sending the Son — is the ultimate ground of all education. Human parents and teachers are not simply training children for civic life; they are cooperating with the divine Pedagogue who is already forming every soul toward His own likeness. To neglect a child's formation is not merely a social failure but a failure to cooperate with God's own work.

### Internal Tensions and Caveats

1. **Tension between openness and rigorism**: Clement accepts Greek philosophy as preparation for Christianity, yet his behavioral prescriptions in Books II–III govern even how loudly one may laugh, whether to use a perfumed bath, or what kind of sandal is appropriate.
2. **The gnostic problem**: Writing against Christian gnostics who claimed higher spiritual knowledge inaccessible to ordinary believers, Clement's Paedagogus/Didaskalos distinction risks reproducing the elitism he criticizes, even as he democratizes gnosis.
3. **Class and gender**: Like Chrysostom, Clement assumes a wealthy urban Alexandrian audience. The formation of the poor is largely invisible.
4. **Philosophical synthesis**: Clement relies heavily on Musonius Rufus, the Stoics, and Plato. Critics question how deeply Christian his ethics is beneath the philosophical veneer.
