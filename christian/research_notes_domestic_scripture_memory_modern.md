## 1. The Domestic Church (*Ecclesia Domestica*) — The Family as the Primary Site of Christian Formation

### Summary

The concept of the home as a "little church" is patristic, rooted in the New Testament itself. Paul's letters refer to congregations meeting "in the house of" named individuals (Romans 16:5; 1 Corinthians 16:19; Colossians 4:15), and the fourth-century Fathers built an entire theology of family formation on this foundation. John Chrysostom is the most prolific and direct voice on this theme. He does not use the exact phrase *ecclesia domestica* but is explicit that the household *is* a church in miniature, with the father functioning as its bishop. His teaching assigns the father three specifically episcopal duties: leading household Scripture reading, organizing prayer, and conducting catechetical instruction of children. Augustine, approaching from a different angle, saw the domestic church as flowing from Baptism itself — the baptized household automatically constitutes a small church — while Chrysostom emphasized that the home must be *actively made* into a church through deliberate parental effort. The modern assumption that religious formation is the school's or parish's job is, from this patristic vantage point, a serious inversion: for Chrysostom, the parish church exists to supply material (homilies, readings) which the father then "brings home" and teaches to his family.

### Direct Quotes from Primary Texts

**Chrysostom, Homily 20 on Ephesians** (the locus classicus; fetched from newadvent.org/fathers/230120.htm):
> "Govern thy wife, and thus will the whole house be in harmony...If we thus regulate our own houses, we shall be also fit for the management of the Church. For indeed **a house is a little Church.**"
*(Homilies on Ephesians, Homily 20; Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers, First Series, Vol. 13, trans. Gross Alexander; Philip Schaff, ed., 1889)*

**Chrysostom, Commentary on Genesis (Second Sermon):**
> "In short, the household might become a church, so that the devil is driven off and that evil spirit, the enemy of our salvation, takes to flight."

**Chrysostom, Homily 20 on Ephesians** (on the father's teaching duty):
> "Let the husband ask his wife at home...the account of the things which were said and read there."

**Augustine, Sermon 94** (on the episcopal duty of household heads):
> "To every man, then, if he is the head of his own house, ought the office of the Episcopate to belong, to take care how his household believe, that none of them fall into heresy."

## 2. Scripture as Curriculum — The Psalms, Memorization, and *Lectio Divina*

### Summary

Chrysostom's *De Inani Gloria* prescribes a complete program of Scripture-based formation beginning in infancy. The child's soul is a city, with the five senses serving as its gates, and the parent as the king who governs those gates. Chrysostom recommends parents tell biblical stories during leisure time, beginning with Cain and Abel and Jacob and Esau, presented as narratives — "Once upon a time there were two sons of one father" — rather than formal lessons. He recommends teaching children to sing the Psalms "so full of love of wisdom." The Desert Fathers made memorization of the entire Psalter a prerequisite for entering monastic life; for them, the Psalms were not a text to be studied but an atmosphere to be inhabited. *Lectio divina* ("sacred reading") as a formal practice was codified by Benedict in his Rule, drawing on Origen's methods. It was formalized into four sequential steps by the Carthusian monk Guigo II in the 12th century in his *Scala Claustralium* (*The Ladder of Monks*): *lectio* (reading), *meditatio* (meditation), *oratio* (prayer), *contemplatio* (contemplation). This is not rote memorization: the goal is for the text to descend from the lips into the heart, reshaping desire and attention.

### Direct Quotes from Primary Texts

**Chrysostom, *De Inani Gloria*, §39** (on biblical storytelling):
> "Let them not hear such tales. But when the boy takes relaxation from his studies — for the soul delights to dwell on stories of old — speak to him, drawing him away from all childish folly; for thou art raising a philosopher and athlete and citizen of Heaven."
*(De Inani Gloria, trans. M.L.W. Laistner, 1951)*

**Chrysostom, *Homilies on Colossians*, Homily 9** (on the Psalms):
> "Teach him to sing those psalms which are so full of love of wisdom."

**Chrysostom, *De Inani Gloria*, §50** (on naming):
> "So let the name of the saints enter our homes through the naming of our children, to train not only the child but the father..."

**Chrysostom, *Homilies on 1 Timothy*, Homily 9** (on neglect of children):
> "We take care of our possessions for our children, but of the children themselves we take no care at all."

**Jerome** (on Scripture memorization for monks):
> "Always keep in your hands and beneath your eyes the Bible, learning the Psalter word for word, praying unceasingly, keeping your mind in an alert state and not open to vain thoughts."

**Guigo II, *Scala Claustralium*** (on the four steps of lectio divina, 12th c.):
The four steps: *lectio* (reading), *meditatio* (meditation), *oratio* (prayer), *contemplatio* (contemplation) — described as "the first description of methodical prayer in the western mystical tradition."

## 3. Memory in Augustine and Hugh of Saint Victor — Memory as the Seat of the Self

### Summary

Augustine's tenth book of the *Confessions* is the most philosophically ambitious meditation on memory in the ancient world. Memory, for Augustine, is not a filing cabinet but a living landscape — "the fields and spacious palaces of my memory" — containing not just images but the learned sciences, emotional traces, and the capacity for self-reflection. Most strikingly, Augustine argues that God is found *within* memory: the soul's encounter with Truth is not an external event but an interior discovery. Hugh of Saint Victor applied a related vision to the practice of reading in his *Didascalicon*. For Hugh, reading without memory is reading without benefit: "The whole usefulness of education consists only in the memory of it." Both thinkers represent a tradition in which education is not information-transfer but the shaping of an interior world. Where modern education delivers content to be assessed and forgotten, the patristic and medieval model insisted on *retention as formation*: what a person truly knows is what has become part of them, reshaping how they love, perceive, and act.

### Direct Quotes from Primary Texts

**Augustine, *Confessions*, Book 10, Chapter 8** (memory as palace; fetched from newadvent.org/fathers/110110.htm):
> "I come to the fields and spacious palaces of my memory, where are the treasures of innumerable images, brought into it from things of all sorts perceived by the senses."

**Augustine, *Confessions*, Book 10, Chapter 8** (self-encounter in memory):
> "In it I meet myself as well. I remember myself and what I have done, when and where I did it, and the state of my mind."

**Augustine, *Confessions*, Book 10, Chapter 14** (the belly of the mind):
> "The memory is, as it were, the belly of the mind, and joy and sadness, like sweet and bitter food...when entrusted to the memory, are, as it were, passed into the belly, where they may be stored but cannot taste."

**Augustine, *Confessions*, Book 10, Chapter 8** (memory's vastness exceeds the self):
> "Great is this force of memory, excessive great, O my God...Therefore is the mind too strait to contain itself."

**Augustine, *Confessions*, Book 10, Chapter 25** (God found within memory):
> "Since then I learnt Thee, Thou residest in my memory; and there do I find Thee, when I call Thee to remembrance."

**Hugh of Saint Victor, *Didascalicon*** (restoring the divine image):
> "This, then, is what the arts are concerned with, this is what they intend, namely, to restore within us the divine likeness."

**Hugh of Saint Victor, *Didascalicon*** (memory over mere reading):
> "Therefore I beg you, reader, not to rejoice too greatly if you have read much, but if you have understood much. Nor that you have understood much, but that you have been able to retain it."

**Hugh of Saint Victor, *Didascalicon*** (learn everything):
> "Learn everything. Later you will see that nothing is superfluous."

**Hugh of Saint Victor, *Didascalicon*** (on exile as a condition for true learning):
> "The man who finds his homeland sweet is still a tender beginner; he to whom every soil is as his native one is already strong; but he is perfect to whom the entire world is as a foreign land."

## 4. Modern Recovery of Patristic Educational Ideals

### Summary

The modern classical Christian education movement has roots in Dorothy Sayers' 1947 Oxford lecture "The Lost Tools of Learning," which argued that medieval education's Trivium trained students *how to think* rather than merely transmitting information. The practical movement launched with Douglas Wilson's *Recovering the Lost Tools of Learning* (1991) and the founding of Logos School (Moscow, Idaho, 1981). David Hicks' *Norms and Nobility: A Treatise on Education* (1981) provided the philosophical grounding: classical education is "a spirit of inquiry and a form of instruction concerned with the development of style through language and of conscience through myth," aiming to produce a "noble type" who knows what is good and serves it above self. The movement's third generation engaged Augustinian themes directly through James K.A. Smith's *Desiring the Kingdom* (2009) and *You Are What You Love* (2016), which argue — drawing explicitly on Augustine — that humans are fundamentally *lovers* rather than *thinkers*, and that formation operates through embodied practices (liturgies) rather than intellectual instruction alone. Stratford Caldecott's *Beauty in the Word* (2012) recast the Trivium as Remembering, Thinking, and Communicating, placing memory — in the Augustinian sense — at the foundation of all genuine learning.

### Key Figures and Works

**Dorothy Sayers** — "The Lost Tools of Learning" (1947): founding text arguing for recovery of the medieval Trivium as cognitive formation rather than content delivery.

**David Hicks** — *Norms and Nobility* (1981):
> "The goal of a classical education is the cultivation of the human spirit: to teach the young to know what is good, to serve it above self, and to recognize that in this knowledge lies that responsibility."

**Douglas Wilson** — *Recovering the Lost Tools of Learning* (1991): practical manifesto launching the contemporary classical Christian school movement.

**James K.A. Smith** — *Desiring the Kingdom* (2009) and *You Are What You Love* (2016):
> "What if instead of starting from the assumption that human beings are thinking things, we started from the conviction that human beings are first and foremost lovers? What if you are defined not by what you know but by what you desire?"
*(You Are What You Love, p. 1)*

> "You can't think your way to holiness. If you could, then a PhD would be a route to sainthood."
*(Smith, interview, The Gospel Coalition)*

**Stratford Caldecott** — *Beauty in the Word: Rethinking the Foundations of Education* (Angelico Press, 2012): recasts Trivium as Remembering/Thinking/Communicating; Grammar stage is fundamentally about *memory* as the foundation of selfhood and culture; draws on Augustine, Hugh of Saint Victor, and the Catholic sacramental tradition.

**Association of Classical Christian Schools (ACCS)**: Founded 1994; now coordinates over 500 member schools in North America.

### Notable Caveats

- The classical Christian education movement is almost entirely Protestant in institutional organization (ACCS), though its patristic sources are shared with Catholic and Orthodox traditions. Caldecott and some other key thinkers are Catholic.
- Smith's *You Are What You Love* (2016) is a popular adaptation of the more academic trilogy (*Desiring the Kingdom*, 2009; *Imagining the Kingdom*, 2013). For academic citation, use the trilogy; for a parenting essay, *You Are What You Love* is more accessible.
- Andrew Kern and the CiRCE Institute are prominent practitioners but not academic theologians; their claims about patristic sources should be cross-checked against primary scholarship.
