# Christian Traditional Education — Essay Project

An essay on the Christian tradition's theology and practice of child formation, written as a guide for modern parents. The project covers late antiquity through the medieval period (roughly 300–1400 CE), with primary emphasis on patristic writers who addressed education and formation directly.

## Essay Overview

The essay is organized in ten parts:

1. **A Note Before Beginning** — scope, honest caveats about the tradition's gender and class limitations
2. **What Is Education For?** — the radical difference between the patristic answer and modern defaults; the *telos* of formation
3. **Paideia — The Greek Inheritance and Its Transformation** — Basil's bee and dyer metaphors; what the Fathers kept and transformed
4. **The Child in the Image of God** — the theological anthropology (*imago Dei*, Gregory of Nyssa) that underpins everything
5. **Chrysostom's Complete Program** — a close reading of *De Inani Gloria*: the city/wax/pearl metaphors, the dinner-table method, the five practices, the athlete for Christ
6. **Christ the Inner Teacher** — Augustine's and Aquinas's *De Magistro*: why no human teacher can put knowledge inside another mind, and what follows for parents
7. **Scripture as Curriculum** — the Psalms, the dinner-table storytelling method, *lectio divina* and its four stages
8. **The Domestic Church** — Chrysostom's "a house is a little Church"; the father as bishop of the household; the inversion of modern assumptions
9. **Humility, Obedience, and Wonder** — Benedict's twelve degrees of humility; Hugh of Saint Victor's *Didascalicon* and the *liber mundi*; the encyclopedic vision and the "learn everything" principle
10. **What Modern Parents Can Recover** — honest translation: what travels, what requires the original context
11. **The Telos** — the goal of formation: not achievement or happiness but likeness to God

## Source Notes

All research notes include full citations with URLs where available. Sources were gathered via web search and direct page fetches from New Advent, CCEL, Tertullian.org, Early Christian Writings, OSB Archive, DeSales Aquinas Translation Project, and Kolbe Foundation.

Key primary texts:
- Chrysostom's *De Inani Gloria* quotes confirmed via De Wet (2014, *Oral History Journal of South Africa*) and Christou (2018, *Akropolis*) citing Laistner's 1951 Cornell translation — the archive.org plain-text was inaccessible during research
- Chrysostom's homilies, Clement's *Paedagogus* Book I, Augustine's *Confessions* and homilies, and Benedict's Rule: fetched directly from their online critical editions
- Aquinas *De Magistro*: fetched from DeSales ATP and Kolbe Foundation (McGlynn translation)
- Hugh of Saint Victor's *Didascalicon*: from Jerome Taylor translation (oook.info, credomag.com)

Key scholarly anchors:
- Werner Jaeger, *Early Christianity and Greek Paideia* (Harvard UP, 1961)
- H.I. Marrou, *A History of Education in Antiquity* (U Wisconsin P, 1956)
- Jean Leclercq, O.S.B., *The Love of Learning and the Desire for God* (Fordham UP, 1982)
- Ivan Illich, *In the Vineyard of the Text* (U Chicago P, 1993)
- James K.A. Smith, *You Are What You Love* (Baker Academic, 2016)
